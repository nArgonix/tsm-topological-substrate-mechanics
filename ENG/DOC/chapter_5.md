## 5.1. Classification of Matrix Excitations: Structural Nodes versus Wave Modes

Within the framework of TSM, a key challenge is the ontological distinction between what we macroscopically call "matter" and what we classify as "interaction" or "radiation." In the Standard Model of physics, wave-particle duality is imposed *a priori*. In TSM, this distinction emerges directly from the nonlinear dynamics of the continuous 0-Matrix medium.

Subatomic phenomena are divided into two rigorous mechanical categories:

### 1. Wave Modes (Linear Oscillations)

These are the simplest forms of 0-Matrix excitation, propagating as perturbations of density or local stress within the medium (transverse and longitudinal waves).

* **Characteristics:** Wave modes exhibit linear (or quasi-linear at low amplitudes) characteristics. They transfer energy and momentum but do not carry "topological memory" ($\mathcal{W} = 0$). After the wave passes through a given region, the 0-Matrix structure relaxes back to its ground state.
* **Physical Role:** They are responsible for the propagation of interactions (photons, phonons). They act as background vibrations that have not reached the threshold of topological stability. Their existence is transient—they decay as energy dissipates through the volume of space.

### 2. Structural Nodes (Topological Solitons)

Matter is not a point in a vacuum, but a high-amplitude "trapped vibration." It is a nonlinear excitation state in which energy deforms the local geometry of the Matrix so strongly that it forms a permanent knot.

* **Characteristics:** A node forms when local nonlinearity exceeds the critical plasticity threshold of the medium, forcing a permanent looping of deformation lines (yielding a topological invariant $\mathcal{W} \neq 0$). They are topologically protected and cannot undergo annihilation via simple interference without physically rupturing the continuity of the 4D network.
* **Physical Role:** They represent fermions. This phenomenon resembles a "wrinkle" in a rug which, due to the material's structural resistance, cannot spontaneously smooth itself out.

**Non-linearity Threshold:** The transition from wave to particle is determined by the local energy density. Upon exceeding the critical threshold $E_{\text{crit}}$, nonlinear coupling causes the wave to "fold" onto itself. A particle is therefore a stable structural phenomenon, whereas a wave is a transient state of stress.

where $n_0$ denotes the density of synchronized modes, and $\phi_0$ is the global phase of the system. Such a structure behaves like a single, ideal oscillator, exhibiting zero dissipation of elastic energy (superfluidity).
